## How to Use This Book

This is not a book you read once and shelve. It is a planning instrument.

Read it the first time like a story - the story of why the acceleration is already here, why ordinary households and careers are the real battlefield, and why energy, compute, and human agency become the new strategic assets.

The second time, treat it like an operating system. Run the exercises. Update the probabilities in the live model. Map your real exposure. Identify your first thirty-day sprint. Talk to your family. Talk to your team. Talk to the people who will matter when the old systems get brittle.

The companion model is the point. Static books become obsolete the moment the frontier moves. Use the embedded planner to stress-test your assumptions, then update as reality changes.

The website also embeds the Rehoboam timeline planner. Use it to explore possible post-AGI timelines and societal impacts before you set your own weights. The point is not to outsource judgment to a dashboard. The point is to notice which domains move together: AI capability, robotics, labour markets, energy, compute, policy, social trust, health, education, and the ordinary household machinery of bills and care.

> You do not need to believe in one exact timeline to act. You need to assign probabilities, allocate capital and attention, monitor reality, and update.

This book is written for individuals and leaders. If you are a parent, start with your household. If you are a founder, start with your business model. If you are a CxO, start with workforce redesign and social licence. If you are a public servant, start with transition income, public AI access, legitimacy, and resilience. If you are an educator, start with children's agency and AI fluency. If you are simply trying to get through the month, start with bills, debt, health, and trusted people.

The goal is not ideological purity. The goal is adaptive positioning. The goal is not to predict the future. The goal is to participate in its construction.

## Opening: The Future Stopped Arriving Politely

There are moments when the future stops arriving politely.

At first, the change looks like a better tool. A faster search engine. A chatbot that writes emails. A coding assistant that saves a few hours. A model that reads a policy document, summarises a board pack, builds a prototype, writes code, or tutors your child with infinite patience.

Then the slope changes.

The tool becomes a colleague. The colleague becomes a team. The team becomes an automated company. The automated company starts competing with human organisations that still hire, coordinate, approve, meet, report, and sleep.

That is the feeling many people are noticing now. It is not simply that AI is getting better. It is that the time between impossible, demo, product, and normal is collapsing. A capability that looked like a research frontier becomes a product. A product becomes a workflow. A workflow becomes an expectation. Then everyone pretends it was obvious all along.

The old planning model assumes society gives us time. Study. Get a job. Buy insurance. Save for retirement. Raise children. Trust that institutions will absorb disruption over decades, ideally with a committee, a consultation paper, and a PDF nobody reads.

That model worked when technology changed tools faster than it changed labour itself. AGI changes the planning assumption. If AI can perform most economically useful cognitive work, and robotics can eventually perform much of the physical work, then labour income, education, taxation, pensions, insurance, politics, social status, and purpose all move.

The disruption will not arrive evenly. Some people will experience it as a productivity boom. Some will experience it as a hiring freeze. Some will see their business margin vanish while the website still says "we value our people". Some will get richer because they own energy, compute, land, infrastructure, capital, or AI-native companies. Some will be told to reskill after the ladder they were climbing has already been quietly removed.

### The shift beneath the shift

The obvious story is that AI automates tasks. That is true, but incomplete. The deeper story is that AI changes the cost of intelligence. When the cost of intelligence falls, coordination changes. When coordination changes, organisations change. When organisations change, labour markets change. When labour markets change, households, governments, and communities change.

This is why the planning lens has to be broader than career advice. You cannot solve a civilisation-level shift with a new LinkedIn headline.

In my post-AGI planning frame, preparation has three layers.

- Financial: income, debt, savings, assets, insurance, and retirement assumptions.
- Physical: shelter, energy, food, water, health, communications, transport, tools, and practical capacity.
- Social: family, trusted people, institutions, community, governance, status, and purpose.
Those layers interact. A household with money but no practical resilience can still be fragile. A community with goodwill but no energy, food, tools, or plan can still be overwhelmed. A person with AI skills but no trusted network can still be isolated. A country with clever models but broken housing supply can still descend into scarcity politics and yell at the wrong spreadsheet.

AGI planning begins when these layers are treated as one system.

### The leader's question

Leaders should read this book with one question in mind: how do we make this transition better for humanity, not just more efficient for balance sheets?

That means pushing for abundance instead of scarcity, resilience instead of dependence, dignity instead of abandonment, and human enhancement instead of passive displacement. It means taking seriously the possibility that energy, compute, land, water, robotics, health, and trusted communities become strategic assets.

It also means rejecting two lazy stories. The first is doom without agency: everything is going to collapse, so the only rational move is retreat. The second is optimism without infrastructure: everything will be abundant, so planning is unnecessary. Both stories are emotionally convenient. Neither is enough.

The right posture is resilient acceleration. Reduce the fragilities that make people panic. Increase the capabilities that let people build. Protect the household. Strengthen the community. Advocate for the abundant branch. Update in real time.

> We are not outside the event looking in. We are inside the acceleration. The work now is to plan like it.

### Opening Action: Name the Acceleration

Write down three things that changed in the last twelve months:

- A tool that can now do something you assumed required a human.
- A workflow in your job, business, or household that AI can already compress.
- A physical-world capability - robotics, energy storage, drones, sensors, manufacturing, logistics, medicine - that looks closer than it did a year ago.
Then classify each one: does it affect income, prices, assets, debt, or insurance? Does it affect shelter, energy, food, water, health, logistics, or security? Does it affect trust, status, community, education, politics, or purpose?

Finally ask the harder question: if each improves at the same rate for another two years, what breaks first? Your plan begins where your exposure is real.

## Chapter 1: From Scarcity to Abundance - My Why

I did not arrive at post-AGI planning through an abstract debate about whether machines can think.

I came to it through a family story about scarcity, adaptation, migration, work, health, technology, and the strange human habit of crossing borders because tomorrow might be better than yesterday.

I was born in Harbin, Heilongjiang, in the far north-east of China, up near Russia and North Korea - or a rock and a hard place. Not exactly the soft-launch region of civilisation. Harbin is a place of extremes. In winter, the temperature can fall to around minus 40 degrees Celsius, and the city turns ice and snow into a festival because humans are excellent at taking hostile conditions and adding lighting design. Even the province name, Hei Long Jiang, translates as Black Dragon River. Subtle, obviously.

My wife Alyse's family came from Guangzhou in southern China, where the heat pushes the other way, up towards plus 40 degrees Celsius. She was born in the year of the dragon, so I sometimes point out that together we are basically a Song of Ice and Fire, minus the problematic family tree. Ancestry appears to have spared us the worst HBO subplot.

Underneath the joke is the serious point: our families came from places where conditions shaped people. Heat, cold, scarcity, political history, family expectation, and the constant question of whether the next generation could have more room to move.

Both of my parents were the youngest in their families. They were natural risk-takers, willing to move outside the familiar system and test whether a different life was possible. My parents grew up inside scarcity. My father told stories about my grandmother chasing him down the street after he took a whole flour bun from the bamboo steamer, because that bun was meant to feed the family, including his three siblings. That is a very efficient childhood lesson in marginal utility. It also explains why immigrant parents can look at a full pantry and still behave like civilisation is one bad week away from auditing the rice bag.

My mother's family lost property, land, and a paint shop business during the Cultural Revolution. That is a direct education in the difference between a system that equalises by removing ownership and a society that expands opportunity by increasing abundance.

My grandfather on his side was a hair dresser who got put into the prisoner's camps during the Japanese invasion and worked in the coal mines, before being rescued by the People's Liberation Army under the Communist Party.

My father studied English and worked as a translator and tour guide in Harbin. That work opened the door to the wider world. He translated for visiting photographers, including Bruce Dale, the National Geographic photographer, who later sponsored him to come to Australia. My mother also studied English and worked at an export textiles company. She travelled for work. My father travelled as a translator. They saw beyond the system they grew up in and decided the best chance for our family was to find opportunity abroad.

In 1992 my father came to Tasmania as an international student. In 1993, after almost a year living apart, my mother and I joined him. I was seven years old, starting again in Hobart, finishing Year 1 at South Hobart Primary School. Six months later we moved to Sydney.

### Technology as both ladder and wave

Australia was not an instant success story. My parents sold textiles at markets, washed dishes, took odd jobs, and saved what they could — the classic migrant scramble for a foothold.

In 1996 they opened Wonderland World Travel. I still remember how excited they were. They bought a Macintosh LC575 (the first computer I fell in love with), rented an office in the city, painted “WONDERLAND WORLD TRAVEL” on the wall, and hung photos from their travels. Walk-in customers came. They printed orders. They had their first paying clients.

Then the internet arrived.

What looked like a sensible business — my father translating and booking, my mother handling export and logistics — was suddenly competing with a new coordination layer. Online booking made everything transparent and cheaper. Margins collapsed. Small agencies were squeezed. That was my first real lesson in technological disruption: not a chart, but a family business being quietly routed around.

My father became a taxi driver. Then Uber arrived and did it again — platforms reorganising demand, pricing, and reputation while the old system watched its value evaporate.

Now AI and robotics are coming for the next layer, from robotaxis to digital workers to humanoid robots. My dad is just glad he’s retired and won’t have to reinvent himself again.

### Childhood Adaptations

After South Hobart Primary School, I jumped to Lindfield Primary as that was the first place my folks found a place to live in Sydney. It was a community house with friendly guests — I remember the guy who rented the movie *Short Circuit* from Blockbuster, and I was intrigued by how cool robots could be. We stayed in Lindfield for about a year before moving to Leichhardt. Looking back, I always wondered why *Looking for Alibrandi* was such a popular book in the library there. I loved Leichhardt; my classmates were all really friendly.

After a year there, we moved to Cammeray, which wasn’t as friendly — I was probably only the second Asian guy there and got the nickname “flat face” (which, to be fair, is pretty accurate). I had to adapt to some of the bullies there, both verbally and physically.

My favourite primary school was Burwood for Year 5 and 6, because that place was starting to feel more China-friendly (and now is pretty much China). They had dozens of LC575s in the school to play sample discs I got from computer magazines like *Mac Addict*, with fully playable demos like Doom on them. So much possibility, and nerding out about the internet with my classmates.

So after five primary schools in five years, I got to experience change as normality.

It also made high school at Sydney Tech, where I stayed for six years, a lot more appreciable. Despite being a boys’ school, the nerds were able to take over the computers in the workshop and LAN up Counter-Strike to play during lunch. This was also when online forums started spinning up and we all found ways to connect with people on the other side of the world doing the same thing. I was even able to easily get my own website and build Flash games myself, inspiring so many possibilities to get your message out and share experiences with anyone online.

### The respectable ladder is not immune

I later joined Deloitte in business tax advisory. It was the kind of serious professional path migrant families are told to aim for: structured work, complex rules, client trust, analysis, compliance, judgment, prestige. In other words, the respectable ladder. The one everyone tells you is safe because it has a logo on the building and people wear shoes that make meetings sound important.

But the more I looked at the work, the more obvious the automation curve became. Tax advisory is full of patterns: read legislation, interpret rules, compare facts, search precedent, draft advice, check edge cases, prepare workpapers, explain risk. Some of it still needs accountability, judgment, and human trust. I am not suggesting we let a chatbot freestyle tax law after three confident paragraphs and a hallucinated case citation. But a large share of the cognitive workflow is exactly the kind of thing AI gets better at when models can read, reason across documents, call tools, and produce structured outputs.

That realisation pushed me deeper into AI. At Deloitte I started branching into technology, lean startups, data analytics, automation, and AI solutions. I worked around strategic foresight connected to Singularity University and helped build communities around emerging technology, transhumanism, and policy. Later I led teams at KPMG to build and take these solutions to market, while also building startup and network-state experiments. Since late 2023, my work at Microsoft has focused on accelerating AI adoption for the education industry.

That arc matters because it is the same arc as the family story. We moved from scarcity to opportunity, from manual work to knowledge work, from a travel agency computer to AI systems that reason across domains, and from trying to fit into existing institutions to trying to help build better ones.

### Why abundance is personal

This is why I am suspicious of scarcity thinking. Scarcity thinking tells us to pull back demand, shut doors, blame immigrants, protect old bottlenecks, and accept that housing, health, education, and opportunity must remain expensive because there is not enough to go around.

The better answer is to increase supply while helping people through the transition. Build more. Automate more. Coordinate better. Use technology to make the pie bigger instead of holding an increasingly dramatic meeting about who gets the last slice.

Legal immigrants and international students are often treated as demand pressure in a spreadsheet. But my family was not just demand. We became workers, students, small-business owners, taxpayers, community members, customers, builders, and eventually contributors to the technology ecosystem. A serious society should not respond to demand by closing doors. It should respond by building enough supply that opportunity is not rationed through scarcity.

### The hospital bed and the transhumanist turn

There is also a more personal reason this matters. I was once robbed in Malaysia and woke up in hospital with tubes in my body everywhere. You do not need a philosophy degree to understand mortality when you wake up like that. Life stops being an abstraction. Fragility becomes physical.

That experience clarified something for me. If I had been 80 years old in that hospital bed but still healthy, conscious, curious, connected, and able to keep living well, I would still have wanted to continue. I do not see virtue in death simply because a calendar says someone has reached a socially accepted age. I care about health, agency, relationships, consciousness, and the ability to keep participating in the world.

That is why transhumanism matters to me. It is not about escaping humanity. It is about taking human limitations seriously enough to improve them. We should use science and technology to extend healthy lifespan, enhance intelligence, improve wellbeing, and give people more agency over the conditions of their lives.

Post-AGI planning is not a side project for me. It is where the family story, the technology story, and the transhumanist story finally point at the same thing. My parents crossed a literal border because Australia represented possibility. The post-AGI border will be less visible but just as real. People will cross from labour income to AI-amplified work, from old credentials to new capabilities, from scarcity politics to supply-side abundance, from biological fatalism to healthy-longevity strategy, and from national institutions to more networked forms of coordination.

> Behind every model, benchmark, robot demo, market forecast, and policy argument are families trying to live well.

### Chapter 1 Action: Write Your Why

Before you build a post-AGI plan, write down why the future matters to you. Do not start with the technology. Start with the people.

- Who are you trying to protect?
- What scarcity shaped your life or family?
- What opportunity changed your trajectory?
- What human limitation do you most want technology to help overcome?
- What kind of abundance would actually improve your life?
- What future would you regret failing to help build?
If your why is only "AI is interesting", it will not survive volatility. If your why is family, health, dignity, freedom, community, or human potential, planning becomes less abstract.

## Chapter 2: The Abundance Engine

The most important question about AGI is not whether the machine can win an argument about consciousness on the internet.

The most important question is this: what happens when intelligence becomes a utility?

Electricity changed every industry because energy could be distributed. The internet changed every industry because information could be distributed. AGI changes every industry because cognition can be distributed. Robotics changes every industry because physical agency can be distributed.

Put those together and you get the abundance engine: intelligence, energy, robotics, compute, networks, biology, and human coordination converging into a new productive layer for civilisation.

This is the optimistic branch, and it deserves to be taken seriously. The world has grand challenges: food, water, shelter, energy, education, healthcare, freedom, safety, longevity, and meaning. For most of history, we attacked those problems with scarce human expertise, slow institutions, limited capital, and weak coordination. AGI changes the leverage. It lets us apply intelligence to every bottleneck at once.

### The Six Ds of post-AGI planning

A useful way to understand exponential technologies is the Six Ds: digitisation, deception, disruption, demonetisation, dematerialisation, and democratisation. For post-AGI planning, translate that into ordinary life.

The Six Ds explain why the transition can feel boring right before it becomes obvious. A technology is deceptive when it is improving faster than social imagination. People look at the current version and say it is not good enough. Then it improves. Then it improves again. Then the argument changes from "it cannot do the job" to "why is anyone still paying that much for the job?"

AI is the first wave because bits are easy to move. Robotics is the second because atoms are harder. But once the intelligence layer connects to machines, sensors, logistics, energy, and manufacturing, the same dematerialising pressure starts moving into the physical economy.

Bits become cheap first. Atoms follow.

### Why abundance is not magic

Abundance is not a mood. It is not a motivational poster with solar panels. It is not a billionaire telling people who cannot afford rent that everything will be fine once the robots arrive.

Abundance is a supply strategy. It means increasing the production and distribution of the goods and services that make life better: housing, energy, food, water, healthcare, education, compute, mobility, safety, creative tools, and time. If AI makes software abundant but housing remains scarce, people still live in scarcity. If healthcare diagnostics become brilliant but access remains bottlenecked, people still suffer. If energy is constrained, compute becomes constrained. If compute is constrained, access to intelligence becomes unequal.

The abundance engine needs physical infrastructure. You cannot prompt your way into a functioning substation. You cannot prompt your way into a million new homes without planning approvals, materials, trades, machines, energy, logistics, financing, and trust. AI can accelerate each of those, but only if leaders treat supply as the mission.

This is where post-AGI planning becomes different from normal futurism. We are not only asking what technology can do. We are asking what must be built so the benefits reach actual households.

### The abundance stack

A practical abundance stack has seven layers.

1. Intelligence: AI models, agents, tutors, copilots, research systems, design systems, and decision tools.
2. Compute: cloud platforms, GPUs, chips, data centres, edge devices, connectivity, and access rights.
3. Energy: generation, storage, transmission, microgrids, electrification, and resilience.
4. Robotics: embodied labour in warehouses, factories, farms, construction, elder care, transport, home services, and maintenance.
5. Biology and health: diagnostics, drug discovery, longevity science, personalised medicine, and continuous health monitoring.
6. Materials and manufacturing: additive manufacturing, modular construction, autonomous production, recycling, and logistics.
7. Community and governance: trust, legitimacy, public access, safety, transition income, ownership models, and local coordination.
When those layers converge, the cost of essentials can fall dramatically. When they are controlled by a few actors, abundance can still be distributed like scarcity. The moral question is therefore also an engineering question: how do we build systems where intelligence abundance becomes human abundance?

> Abundance is not guaranteed by capability. Abundance is produced by capability plus infrastructure plus distribution plus trust.

### Chapter 2 Action: Map Your Abundance Engine

Pick one domain that affects your life directly: housing, health, education, food, energy, work, transport, or community safety.

For that domain, list the bottlenecks across the abundance stack: intelligence, compute, energy, robotics, biology, materials, governance, and trust. Then ask: which bottleneck could AI reduce first? Which bottleneck becomes more valuable? Which bottleneck could I own, access, improve, or advocate for?

## Chapter 3: Who Needs a Plan? The Human Stack

Most people do not plan their lives around technological singularities. They plan around bills.

Can I pay rent or the mortgage? Keep my job? Afford health care? Raise children? Support parents? Invest? Retire? Still have enough time and energy to live a meaningful life?

That is exactly why AGI matters.

AGI is not an abstract debate about whether machines can think. It is a pressure wave moving through the human stack of needs.

### The four layers

At the base are survival needs: food, water, shelter, energy, safety, health, communications. Above that are economic needs: income, savings, insurance, debt, assets, retirement, business continuity. Above that are social needs: family, belonging, local trust, institutions, governance. At the top are human potential needs: learning, creativity, contribution, healthspan, intelligence amplification, and purpose.

AGI touches every layer. That is why the book is not written only for technologists. It belongs to anyone with bills to pay, people to protect, assets to allocate, or a community to lead.

### The ordinary is the battlefield

For an individual, the immediate questions are practical. What happens to my income if AI can do most of my day job? What happens to my savings if markets reprice labour, software, energy, robotics, land, and compute? What happens to retirement if the old economy changes faster than expected? What happens to health and life insurance when longevity science and medical AI improve? What happens to my children's education if the career paths we are preparing them for are automated before they graduate?

For a small business, the questions become existential. What happens when an AI-native competitor operates with a tenth of the cost base? What happens when customers expect instant service, personalised products, automated support, and lower prices? What happens when the founder with agents can do the work of a department?

For leaders, the questions are bigger. How do we protect people during labour displacement without freezing technological progress? How do we share the benefits of abundance? How do we keep critical infrastructure running through social volatility? How do we govern AI systems that mediate knowledge, labour, finance, defence, science, and culture? How do we give people a path to agency instead of turning them into spectators?

### The physical layer still matters

Digital people often underweight the physical layer. They imagine AGI as software eating software, which is a very online way to forget that humans still require rooms, calories, sleep, medicine, clean water, transport, energy, and safety.

Communities need roads, trades, clinics, warehouses, workshops, farms, substations, emergency services, and people who can coordinate when systems fail. Cheaper intelligence can write a plan for abundance. Physical abundance still needs energy, machines, land, materials, logistics, regulation, maintenance, and trust.

Immigration is a good example. When a country faces housing pressure, overloaded services, and cost-of-living stress, blaming legal immigrants can feel politically easy because demand is visible. But supply failure is the deeper problem. If we do not build enough homes, infrastructure, health capacity, schools, energy, transport, and productive automation, every new person looks like a burden. If we accelerate supply with AI, robotics, construction technology, energy buildout, better approvals, and better coordination, people become contributors to abundance rather than contestants in scarcity.

That is the difference between scarcity politics and abundance planning.

### An adaptive plan, not a bunker fantasy

The point is not that every household needs a bunker. Most people do not need a cinematic bunker. They need liquidity, useful skills, reliable people, practical redundancy, and enough AI literacy to stop outsourcing their future to whoever has the most confident newsletter.

Every household needs an adaptive plan. That plan should cover income resilience, essential expenses, debt exposure, insurance assumptions, shelter, food, water, energy, health, communications, children's education, asset allocation, compute access, community support, political advocacy, and update triggers.

AGI forces us to rethink the ordinary. The bills still matter. The family still matters. The job still matters. The business still matters. The community still matters. What changes is the planning horizon.

### Chapter 3 Action: Audit the Human Stack

Map your exposure across four layers: survival, economic, social, and potential.

- What is fragile?
- What is defensible?
- What can AI improve?
- What would break if income dropped for six months?
- What would become more valuable if AI accelerated?
- Who would you call first in a disruption?
Do this as a household, not just as an individual. The singularity will arrive in family calendars before it arrives in official language.

## Chapter 4: The New Currency - Energy, Compute, and Productive Capacity

Money is a claim on future goods and services. In a post-AGI world, the question is: what produces those goods and services?

Increasingly, the answer is energy plus compute plus robotics plus raw materials plus coordination. That is why energy and compute become strategic currencies.

Compute turns electricity into intelligence. Intelligence turns plans into designs, code, simulations, logistics, drug candidates, lesson plans, construction schedules, legal drafts, financial models, robot policies, and autonomous workflows. Robotics turns intelligence into action in the physical world. Energy powers both.

If bits become commoditised by AI, atoms are next. But atoms are stubborn. They need energy, machines, land, logistics, materials, sensors, permits, maintenance, and human trust. The post-AGI world may be abundant, but abundance has infrastructure.

### The cloud perspective

This is not abstract for me. Coming from the experience of Cloud and AI platforms for public sector and education, and from deep familiarity with the Azure business on cloud computing for AI, I see AI less as a single product and more as an industrial stack.

Models sit on compute. Compute sits on data centres. Data centres sit on energy, cooling, networking, chips, capital, supply chains, and regulation. AI adoption sits on procurement, trust, security, skills, integration, change management, and leadership. A school, hospital, agency, company, or community does not become AI-native because someone bought a chatbot licence. It becomes AI-native when intelligence is embedded into workflows, governance, measurement, and culture.

That is why the people who understand the stack will see the bottlenecks before everyone else. It is not enough to ask which model is best. Ask: where is compute constrained? Where is power constrained? Which workflows are ready for agents? Which data is accessible? Which leaders can move? Which sectors have both the need and the permission to change?

### Energy as the floor of abundance

Energy is the floor of abundance. Cheap energy makes compute cheaper. Cheap compute makes intelligence cheaper. Cheap intelligence makes design, science, automation, and coordination cheaper. Better coordination makes energy deployment faster. This loop is powerful.

But if energy is constrained, the loop slows. Data centres compete with households, industry, and electrification. Grid delays become AI delays. Energy prices become intelligence prices. Countries with abundant clean energy, reliable grids, and permission to build gain strategic leverage.

At the household level, this does not mean everyone needs to become an energy trader. It means you should understand your energy dependence. What powers your home? What happens during outages? What is your exposure to rising energy prices? Could solar, batteries, efficiency, backup systems, electric vehicles, or community energy increase your resilience? Which local institutions would remain functional if the grid was stressed?

### Compute as access to intelligence

Compute is access to intelligence. In the old economy, access to expertise was limited by geography, credentials, cost, and networks. In the AI economy, access to intelligence depends on tools, platforms, models, compute availability, data rights, policy, and literacy.

Universal Compute may become as important as universal education. If only a small number of companies and governments can access frontier intelligence, then abundance concentrates. If individuals, schools, communities, startups, public agencies, and small businesses can access powerful tools safely and affordably, the productive base widens.

This is the practical version of democratisation. It is not enough for a model to exist. People need usable access, trusted interfaces, skills, safeguards, and the confidence to build with it.

### Productive infrastructure

A home, farm, workshop, school, clinic, community hub, or business that can generate power, run compute, grow food, repair equipment, coordinate people, and deploy AI-native tools has strategic value. Not because everyone needs to become a prepper. Because the physical world is where the invoices eventually land.

Productive infrastructure is the opposite of passive consumption. It is the capacity to make, fix, coordinate, teach, care, grow, power, compute, and adapt.

In a fast-abundance future, productive infrastructure lets you participate in upside. In a disorderly transition, it reduces fragility. In a gentle singularity, it compounds slowly. In a long-horizon future, it is still useful. In an existential-risk future, it is part of defensive capacity.

> Energy and compute are not just inputs to AI. They are claims on future capability.

### Chapter 4 Action: Build Your Energy + Compute Map

Answer these questions for your household or organisation:

- How dependent are you on stable grid power, cloud access, mobile networks, and digital payments?
- What would fail first in a 48-hour outage or connectivity disruption?
- What AI tools do you use daily, and what would you lose if access disappeared?
- Do you have backup power, backup communications, offline copies of critical documents, and practical repair capacity?
- Where could energy, compute, or productive infrastructure become an asset rather than a bill?
Do not try to solve everything. Identify the first bottleneck that would matter in your real life.

## Chapter 5: Work After Work

The question is not whether AI will affect work. The question is which parts of work remain human, which parts become automated, and which parts become more valuable because AI exists.

A person may not lose their job because AI replaces them in one clean event. They may lose bargaining power because one AI-amplified team can do what three teams used to do. They may lose entry points because junior hiring collapses. They may lose margin because clients expect faster output for lower fees. They may lose identity because work was the structure that held their week together.

The work problem is therefore not only economic. It is psychological, social, and spiritual.

### The ladder breaks before the top disappears

Many professions are built like ladders. Juniors do repetitive work, learn patterns, absorb judgment, and gradually become seniors. If AI automates the repetitive bottom rungs, the profession may still need experts, but it may not know how to grow them. This affects law, consulting, tax, software, finance, design, media, research, medicine, engineering, and education.

The risk is not only unemployment. It is apprenticeship collapse. If the machine handles the tasks that once trained people, we need new ways to create judgment, taste, ethics, responsibility, and trust.

### Defensible human advantage

Do not ask, "What can I learn that AI cannot do?" That question gets smaller every year.

Ask instead: where can I combine AI, human trust, physical-world context, capital, taste, regulation, relationships, mission, and accountability into something defensible?

The future belongs less to people who can perform isolated tasks and more to people who can orchestrate systems. The AI-native worker is not merely faster at email. They are a conductor of agents, tools, data, people, and physical capacity. They know when to delegate to AI, when to verify, when to bring in humans, when to build, when to stop, and when to take responsibility.

### Four work identities

In the transition, many people will move among four work identities.

1. The displaced worker: the old role loses value faster than the person can adapt.
2. The AI-amplified operator: the person uses AI to produce more, faster, and better.
3. The owner of productive assets: the person owns or controls capital, energy, compute, land, infrastructure, robotics, data, distribution, or trusted networks.
4. The human-trust professional: the person is valuable because people trust their judgment, presence, ethics, care, taste, or accountability.
The mistake is to cling to only one identity. Your goal is to build optionality across several. Use AI deeply. Build human trust. Own or access productive assets where possible. Learn physical-world competence. Connect to communities. Develop a mission that can survive job-title volatility.

### Meaning after labour

A world of abundance may solve survival and create a purpose crisis. If Universal High Income arrives and many goods become cheaper, the old bargain - work to survive - weakens. That could be liberating. It could also be disorienting.

Some people will become passive consumers. Others will become creators, builders, carers, explorers, educators, artists, scientists, organisers, founders, and mentors. The difference will not be access to AI alone. It will be purpose, curiosity, discipline, community, and mindset.

This is why children need more than technical skills. They need a Massively Transformative Purpose, curiosity, resilience, agency, and the ability to work with intelligent systems. Education should not be about memorising yesterday's bottlenecks. It should be about forming people who can ask better questions, learn deeply, coordinate with others, care about real humans, and build things worth building.

> The goal is not to be better than AI at being a machine. The goal is to become more human, more capable, and more useful with intelligence at your side.

### Alyse's View: Keep It Simple

My wife Alyse's take is more direct. She does not want to track every model release or join every governance debate. She wants to go to the gym, live her life, and have a simple framework that works.

Her approach has two pillars:

- **Insurance policy**: Accumulate resources through higher-paying work, multiple income streams, equity, and high-growth assets so you have the option to move if things get bad.
- **Basic needs access**: Electricity, water, food, and practical self-reliance - the prepper/homesteader layer that protects you when systems get brittle.

She maps the scenarios bluntly:
- Rapid disruption + civil unrest → Get money and get out.
- Fast abundance → You don't need any of this.
- Slow or long horizon → It's basically now.
- Existential risk → You're dead, do nothing.

She is skeptical of rigid five-year plans ("having a plan is one of the worst things you can do") and prefers a dynamic, macro-level view focused on economics, portfolio allocation, liquidity, and basic resilience. She wants the book to feel like familiar economic thinking rather than new jargon.

This is the voice of the target reader: busy, practical, and unwilling to turn post-AGI planning into a second job.

## Chapter 6: Five Futures, One Portfolio

There is no single post-AGI future. There are scenarios.

This is annoying if you want certainty, but useful if you want to stay alive, solvent, and vaguely sane.

A scenario portfolio prevents two failures. The first is denial: assuming the world continues roughly as it is. The second is monomania: betting everything on one extreme outcome and recruiting your wardrobe, diet, and friendships into the thesis.

### Scenario 1: disorderly labour shock

Starting weight: 20%. AI disrupts the economy faster than institutions can respond. Hiring freezes spread. Roles are decomposed. AI-native competitors attack margins. Junior positions vanish. Debt pressure rises. People feel humiliated, not just unemployed. Governments respond with stimulus cheques, UBI, wage subsidies, emergency transfers, public AI services, compute dividends, or Universal High Income proposals. The politics become messy because pain is uneven.

People do not revolt because a benchmark improves. Nobody storms a parliament because a model got a better score on an eval. They revolt when they cannot pay bills, lose status, lose trust, or believe the system has no place for them. Rapid disruption requires liquidity, essential-needs coverage, defensible income, community trust, and safety.

### Scenario 2: fast abundance

Starting weight: 30%. AI systems rapidly accelerate science, medicine, robotics, energy, education, policy design, and production. Costs fall. Healthcare improves. Education personalises. New income or public provision emerges. Governments and companies coordinate better than expected, which would be a pleasant surprise and should not be excluded purely because it sounds uncharacteristic.

This is the future worth advocating for. But it requires planning before abundance arrives. Cheap intelligence is not the same as a good society. If people carry debt from the old economy, housing remains constrained, health access is uneven, energy and compute are bottlenecked, or communities lose status faster than new purpose forms, the transition can still be chaotic.

### Scenario 3: gentle singularity

Starting weight: 30%. Capabilities improve, but society absorbs them over five to ten years. Regulation slows deployment. Robotics remains harder than software. Enterprises adopt unevenly. Procurement, unions, liability, trust, culture, infrastructure, and politics stretch the transition. Never underestimate the ability of a procurement process to make a miracle technology feel tired.

This gives households, businesses, and governments more time. But slow does not mean safe. A slow takeoff can still destroy careers, widen inequality, and produce geopolitical instability. It can also lull people into underpreparing because each month looks manageable until the cumulative change becomes undeniable.

### Scenario 4: long horizon

Starting weight: 10%. Maybe AGI is decades away. Maybe human intelligence has bottlenecks that are harder to replicate than current models suggest. Maybe society adopts powerful systems slowly. Maybe regulation, hardware, energy, data, economics, or safety constraints delay the full transition.

This scenario matters because overpreparing for a near-term shock can damage your present life if the future takes longer. You still need a career, investments, relationships, health, and meaning. A probabilistic plan keeps you from turning uncertainty into personality.

### Scenario 5: existential risk

Starting weight: 10%. This includes uncontrolled AI systems, engineered pandemics, autonomous weapons escalation, cyber collapse, catastrophic misuse, grey-goo-style nanotechnology, or a self-improving system whose goals diverge from human flourishing. These risks may be lower probability than economic disruption, but their severity is extreme.

We should advocate against them. That means serious safety work, security, monitoring, international coordination, defensive technology, alignment research, biosecurity, cyber resilience, and governance that does not rely on wishful thinking.

### The sixth thread: human merger

Across all scenarios runs a sixth thread: human merger. Humans do not simply compete against AI. We integrate with it. This starts with phones, wearables, AI copilots, memory systems, agents, augmented reality, personalised tutors, medical AI, and cognitive offloading. It deepens through brain-computer interfaces, neurotechnology, continuous health monitoring, AI-mediated communication, and eventually more direct forms of intelligence amplification.

Game theory matters. If some individuals and organisations adopt AI augmentation, others face pressure to adopt just to stay competitive. The ethical challenge is to preserve choice, dignity, privacy, and fairness while recognising that refusing enhancement may become economically difficult.

The best path is not humans versus machines. It is humans becoming more capable with machines while ensuring the gains serve human flourishing.

> Do not average the scenarios into bland advice. Maintain live exposure to the futures that matter.

### Chapter 6 Action: Build Your Scenario Portfolio

Assign your own percentages across the five scenarios. The numbers must add to 100%. For each scenario, write one action for the next 30 days, 6 months, and 2 years.

Then choose the no-regrets actions that help across multiple scenarios: health, AI fluency, liquidity, debt reduction, trusted relationships, energy resilience, productive skills, and community coordination.

## Chapter 7: When - Capability, Deployment, Impact

The AGI timeline debate is noisy because people are answering different questions, often at each other, usually with charts.

Some ask when AI will pass a benchmark. Some ask when it can do a remote worker's job. Some ask when it can operate an entire company. Some ask when robotics can replace physical labour. Some ask when the economy notices. Some ask when politics breaks. Some ask when superintelligence arrives.

Those are not the same date.

### The timeline stack

A better approach is to separate capability from impact.

A frontier model may be capable before enterprises deploy it. Enterprises may deploy it before official labour statistics show the effect. Labour statistics may show the effect before governments understand what to do. Governments may act before communities trust the response. That lag is where both risk and opportunity live.

### Why the near-term frame matters

A near-term AGI horizon is useful because it forces action. If you assume the world may be materially different within one to three years, vague future-proofing stops being enough. You cannot say "I will learn AI someday" or "governments will figure it out" or "my industry is probably safe" and call that a plan. That is not a plan. That is a soothing noise.

A late-2020s estimate should not make you abandon normal life, sell everything, and begin speaking exclusively in probability distributions. It should pull forward work with long lead times: income resilience, AI-native capability, energy and compute exposure, community trust, health, and policy imagination.

The practical question is not whether the forecaster is exactly right. It is whether your plan still works if the pre-AGI era has only a few years left.

### Prediction markets and reality signals

Prediction markets can be useful because they aggregate beliefs, incentives, liquidity, and uncertainty. They are not truth. They can be smarter than you and still wrong. Use them as inputs, not gods.

Reality signals matter more. Update when frontier models improve, agent reliability changes, robotics costs fall, companies replace workflows, governments announce emergency policies, unemployment data shifts, insurance markets adjust, energy and compute markets move, social unrest rises, or safety incidents occur.

Near-term AGI planning is not about being right once. It is about updating faster than the world breaks your old assumptions.

> The timeline is not a date. It is a dashboard.

### Chapter 7 Action: Build Your Watchlist

For each signal, write: what would make me increase my disruption probability? What would make me increase my abundance probability? What would make me act within 30 days?

## Chapter 8: Your 1000-Day Moonshot Plan

A moonshot is not a fantasy. It is a goal large enough to reorganise your behaviour.

Post-AGI planning needs a moonshot because incremental preparation is too slow for exponential change. But the moonshot must be grounded. Your goal is not to predict AGI perfectly. Your goal is to become the kind of person, household, organisation, or community that can adapt as intelligence abundance arrives.

Think in 1000 days. Long enough to build real capacity. Short enough to force action now.

### The MTP of this book

A Massively Transformative Purpose gives direction to effort. For this book, the MTP is: help individuals and leaders survive, thrive, and shape the transition from labour scarcity to intelligence abundance.

Your personal MTP may be different. It might be to protect your family, build a resilient community, create a new AI-native business, improve education, accelerate healthcare, extend healthy life, improve governance, or help people find purpose beyond wage labour. Whatever it is, it should be large enough to pull you through uncertainty.

### Phase 1: 0-30 days - create the first plan

The first thirty days are about visibility and momentum. Run a household or organisational exposure audit. Know your burn rate. List fixed expenses. Identify fragile debt. Review insurance. Map essential needs: shelter, energy, food, water, health, communications, transport. Set up one AI workflow that saves real time each week. Identify one local resilience relationship. Choose one abundance opportunity to learn, build, invest in, or advocate for.

The first plan does not need to be perfect. It needs to exist. An imperfect plan you update beats a perfect plan that lives forever in your head, where all productivity systems go to retire.

### Phase 2: 30-180 days - build capability

Over six months, build capacity. Learn AI deeply. Automate repetitive workflows. Build or join a community of practice. Strengthen savings. Reduce dangerous debt. Improve health habits. Create family documents and contingency plans. Explore energy resilience. Identify where compute access matters in your work. Teach your children or team to use AI as a learning partner rather than an answer vending machine.

If you run a business, redesign one workflow around agents. If you are an educator, redesign one learning experience around curiosity and agency. If you are a public-sector leader, identify one process where AI can improve service delivery without undermining trust. If you are a parent, run a household conversation about what skills matter in a world where memorisation is cheap and agency is priceless.

### Phase 3: 180-365 days - own or access productive assets

The next layer is productive capacity. Can you own or access energy, compute, land, tools, data, distribution, trusted relationships, or specialised knowledge that becomes more valuable as AI accelerates? Can you build a small AI-native business? Can you join a network that compounds your capability? Can you invest in health, education, robotics, energy, biotech, or infrastructure exposure within your risk tolerance?

This is also the point to strengthen community. A group chat is not a community until people can actually help each other. Build relationships with neighbours, builders, farmers, engineers, health workers, educators, founders, local officials, parents, and trusted online networks.

### Phase 4: 365-1000 days - become a node in the better future

By the end of 1000 days, the goal is not just resilience. It is contribution. You should be more AI-fluent, healthier, less fragile, more connected, more useful, and more capable of helping others adapt.

You may have launched an AI-native project, created a local resilience map, helped your school adopt AI safely, supported a policy campaign for Universal Compute, invested in productive infrastructure, or built a community hub. The specific form matters less than the posture: you are no longer waiting for institutions to tell you what the future means. You are participating.

> Do not make a five-year plan for a world that may change in eighteen months. Make a living 1000-day plan that updates monthly.

## Chapter 9: The Distribution Layer - UBI, Universal High Income, and Universal Compute

Abundance only matters if people can access it.

If AI and robotics make goods and services cheaper but the benefits concentrate in a small number of firms, asset owners, or governments, then most people will experience abundance as another version of scarcity. They will watch the future happen on screens while still fighting over rent, healthcare, education, and status.

That is why the distribution layer matters. The three concepts to understand are UBI, Universal High Income, and Universal Compute.

### UBI: the floor

Universal Basic Income is the floor. It provides cash support so people can meet basic needs during labour disruption. It is simple, legible, and fast compared with complex welfare systems. During a rapid transition, governments may reach for cash transfers because they stabilise households faster than retraining programmes can rebuild industries.

But UBI has limits. If the supply of essentials remains constrained, cash transfers can become a more compassionate bidding war. Rent still rises. Healthcare remains bottlenecked. Education remains uneven. Energy remains expensive. A society cannot cash-transfer its way out of a supply problem forever.

### Universal High Income: the aspiration

Universal High Income is the richer idea. It is not just enough money to survive. It is broad access to the goods and services that make life genuinely better: housing, healthcare, education, mobility, energy, AI tutors, creative tools, longevity medicine, cultural participation, and time.

UHI only becomes plausible if AI, robotics, energy, and manufacturing massively expand productive capacity. The point is not that a government prints dignity. The point is that if the abundance engine drives down the cost of essentials and expands supply, a given income can buy far more. A modest cheque in a high-scarcity economy is not transformative. A comparable cheque in a high-abundance economy can be life-changing.

The danger is passivity. A world where people receive enough to consume but lose purpose is not the dream. The better branch pairs universal support with learning, entrepreneurship, caregiving, art, science, community building, health, exploration, and service. Freedom from survival labour should become freedom for contribution, not an invitation to disappear into infinite entertainment.

### Universal Compute: the leverage

Universal Compute may matter as much as income. If intelligence becomes the primary productive tool, then access to compute becomes access to opportunity. Public AI services, school access, community compute credits, open models, secure cloud environments, AI literacy programmes, and low-cost agent platforms could become the next generation of public infrastructure.

Imagine a child in a small town with access to a world-class AI tutor, robotics simulator, science mentor, coding partner, language coach, health education guide, and creative studio. Imagine a small business owner with access to AI operations, accounting, marketing, procurement, legal triage, and design support. Imagine a local council with AI tools for planning approvals, infrastructure modelling, emergency response, translation, community engagement, and budgeting.

That is what democratisation looks like when it is real.

### Distribution requires production

The distribution layer cannot be separated from the production layer. Income support without supply creates inflation pressure. Compute access without energy and data centres becomes symbolic. Public AI without literacy becomes underused or misused. Abundance without trust becomes conflict.

The policy stack should therefore include both sides: transition income, Universal High Income pathways, Universal Compute, energy buildout, housing supply, AI education, health access, robotics deployment, public-sector capacity, data rights, safety governance, and local resilience.

This is where leaders must resist false choices. It is not markets or government. It is markets, government, communities, entrepreneurs, scientists, educators, builders, and citizens working toward a future where intelligence abundance becomes human abundance.

> The post-AGI question is not only who gets money. It is who gets access to intelligence, energy, tools, health, housing, and purpose.

### Chapter 9 Action: Design Your Distribution Thesis

- What supply bottlenecks must be solved so income support does not become inflation?
- What policy would most improve your household or community in the transition?
- What can you advocate for locally, professionally, or politically?
A good distribution thesis is not charity. It is social architecture for a high-capability civilisation.

## Chapter 10: Human Enhancement - Staying in the Game

If intelligence is the new leverage, humans need better access to intelligence.

That sentence is the practical heart of transhumanism. It is not about abandoning the human. It is about refusing to accept every biological limitation as destiny.

For most of history, our bodies and minds were fixed constraints. We had limited memory, limited attention, limited lifespan, limited sensory range, limited communication speed, and limited ability to heal. Technology has always been the story of extending those limits: language, writing, glasses, vaccines, anaesthesia, electricity, computers, smartphones, search engines, prosthetics, pacemakers, cochlear implants, and now AI.

AGI makes enhancement strategic. If machines can learn, reason, design, and act at superhuman speed, humans will face pressure to augment. The ethical challenge is not whether enhancement happens. It is whether enhancement preserves dignity, choice, privacy, fairness, and human flourishing.

### The first merger already happened

The first human-AI merger is not a brain implant. It is the phone in your pocket. It is your calendar, search history, maps, messages, photos, memory, social graph, banking, language translation, and AI assistant. We already outsource memory, navigation, communication, shopping, entertainment, and increasingly judgment to digital systems.

The next layer is AI copilots and agents. They write, code, analyse, schedule, remind, summarise, research, negotiate, and coordinate. Then come wearables, augmented reality, continuous health monitoring, personalised tutors, cognitive offloading, memory systems, and AI-mediated relationships. Brain-computer interfaces may deepen the loop.

Each step raises the same question: does the technology make the person more capable and more free, or more dependent and easier to manipulate?

### Longevity changes planning

Healthy life extension changes everything. If medical AI, diagnostics, drug discovery, regenerative medicine, gene therapies, and personalised health systems extend healthspan, then retirement planning changes. Education changes. Career arcs change. Family planning changes. The value of remaining healthy, curious, and adaptable increases.

I care about this because fragility is not theoretical. Waking up in a hospital bed after violence forces the question: what makes life worth continuing? My answer is not merely survival. It is health, consciousness, agency, relationships, learning, contribution, and the ability to keep participating in the world.

A post-AGI plan should include a health and longevity strategy. Not biohacking theatre. Basics first: sleep, strength, nutrition, metabolic health, relationships, mental health, preventive screening, medical literacy, and access to care. Then add the emerging frontier: personalised diagnostics, AI-assisted health guidance, longevity research, and careful evaluation of new interventions.

### The augmentation ethics

Enhancement creates game theory. If one student uses AI tutors, others may need to. If one worker uses agents, others may need to. If one company uses AI-managed operations, competitors may need to. If some people adopt cognitive augmentation, others may feel forced to choose between privacy and relevance.

The right answer is not to ban progress by default. Nor is it to let every enhancement race happen without guardrails. The right answer is agency: informed consent, privacy, transparency, safety, access, and cultural space for people to choose different levels of augmentation without losing dignity.

Human enhancement should not become another inequality engine. If only elites can access intelligence amplification, longevity medicine, and high-quality AI support, society fractures. If broad access is built into the distribution layer, enhancement becomes part of shared abundance.

> Transhumanism is not the rejection of humanity. It is the decision to take human potential seriously.

### Chapter 10 Action: Create Your Capability Upgrade Plan

- Healthspan: one measurable health habit you will improve.
- Learning: one domain you will explore deeply with AI support.
- Memory and cognition: one system for capturing, retrieving, and compounding your ideas.
- Ethics: one boundary you will protect around privacy, autonomy, or human relationships.
The goal is not to become less human. The goal is to become more capable of protecting and expressing what is human.

## Chapter 11: Leaders - Build the Better Branch

The future is not a weather system. It is a choice architecture.

Technology creates possibility. Leadership determines direction.

If you lead a household, classroom, business, agency, town, school, startup, farm, community, church, research lab, council, department, or country, your task is not merely to adopt AI. Your task is to shape the conditions under which AI transforms human life.

### From scarcity management to abundance leadership

Most institutions are trained to manage scarcity. Allocate limited resources. Control demand. Protect budgets. Reduce risk. Avoid blame. Run consultations. Publish strategies. Move slowly enough that nobody can accuse you of recklessness, even if the world moves anyway.

Abundance leadership asks a different question: how do we increase the supply of what people need and value while protecting dignity, safety, and trust?

That means building housing faster. Deploying energy faster. Expanding healthcare capacity. Using AI in education. Supporting legal migration with infrastructure. Accelerating scientific discovery. Making public services responsive. Giving people access to AI tools. Supporting transition income. Investing in community resilience. Regulating catastrophic risk without freezing beneficial progress.

### The leader's dashboard

A leader needs to track more than productivity. The dashboard should include five categories.

1. Capability: what AI and robotics can now do.
2. Exposure: which jobs, services, workflows, and communities are affected.
3. Resilience: how households, infrastructure, and institutions absorb shocks.
4. Distribution: who gets access to income, compute, education, health, and tools.
5. Meaning: whether people still have agency, purpose, belonging, and dignity.
The danger of AI adoption is that leaders optimise for efficiency while ignoring legitimacy. A technically efficient transition that humiliates people will fail politically. A safe transition that blocks abundance will fail morally. A visionary transition with no operational discipline will fail practically.

The better branch requires all three: speed, safety, and dignity.

### Public sector and education

Public sector and education are central because they are where legitimacy, capability, and the future workforce meet. Schools shape children before labour markets receive them. Governments set rules, distribute support, build infrastructure, and maintain trust. If these institutions lag too far behind the technology curve, households pay the price.

In education, the goal is not to ban AI so students can prove they can still perform obsolete scarcity rituals. The goal is to teach agency, curiosity, judgment, collaboration, scientific literacy, creativity, ethics, and AI fluency. Every child should learn how to ask better questions, verify answers, build with tools, care for people, and pursue a purpose large enough to motivate deep learning.

In government, the goal is not to automate bureaucracy for its own sake. The goal is to deliver better outcomes: faster services, clearer information, better translation, personalised support, fraud detection, policy simulation, emergency response, infrastructure planning, and public trust. AI should make the state more capable and more human, not just more efficient at saying no.

### A call to builders

The most important opportunities of the post-AGI transition will not be only apps. They will be systems that connect intelligence to real human needs: housing, energy, health, education, care, food, water, safety, governance, community, and meaning.

This is where entrepreneurs, policymakers, investors, educators, engineers, artists, health workers, farmers, and citizens can all become builders of the better branch.

The future will not ask whether you were certain. It will ask what you built with the uncertainty you had.

> The best future is not the one we predict. It is the one we organise ourselves to build.

### Chapter 11 Action: Pick Your Leadership Arena

## Conclusion: Do Not Wait for Permission from the Future

The central mistake is waiting for certainty.

By the time everyone agrees that AGI has arrived, the best positioning will already have happened. Careers will already be changing. Companies will already be reorganised. Investment narratives will already have shifted. Policy fights will already be underway. Communities with resilience will already have built trust, energy, food, communications, and coordination capacity.

You do not need certainty to begin. You need a living plan.

Start with the ordinary: bills, income, health, children, family, housing, insurance, debt, retirement, community, and purpose. Then examine each one under near-term AGI assumptions. Do not outsource that thinking to pundits, politicians, employers, or markets. They will be reacting to the same uncertainty.

Then look upward.

If AGI is real, humanity is approaching one of the most important transitions in its history. We can use machine intelligence to accelerate science, extend healthy lifespan, automate dangerous work, educate every child, restore ecosystems, lower the cost of essentials, and expand human potential.

We can build an abundant future.

But abundance is not guaranteed. It has to be built, governed, defended, and distributed.

The post-AGI plan is therefore both personal and civilisational. Protect your household. Upgrade your capabilities. Reduce fragility. Build or join resilient communities. Allocate capital with probabilities. Own or access strategic infrastructure. Learn to work with AI. Watch market and reality signals. Advocate for safety without surrendering to fear. Advocate for abundance without becoming naive. Support human enhancement, healthy longevity, and intelligence amplification so humans are not merely displaced by the tools we create.

That is how to survive and thrive: protect the basics, build capability, keep optionality, coordinate with people you trust, and push the transition toward abundance rather than fear.

> The future is not waiting. Neither should we.
